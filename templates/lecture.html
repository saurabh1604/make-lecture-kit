01 · How machines learn

Gradient Descent

Nearly every model learns the same way: it has a knob, it measures how wrong it is, and it nudges the knob in the direction that reduces the wrongness. Repeat. That loop is gradient descent.

Analogy first. Imagine you are on a foggy hillside and want to reach the lowest point in the valley. You cannot see far, but you can feel which way the ground slopes under your feet. So you take a small step downhill, feel again, and step again. The slope tells you the direction; the size of your step is up to you. Gradient descent is exactly this, on a curve called the loss.

Step 1 — the loss curve

The loss \(L(w)\) is a number that says how wrong the model is for a given setting of the knob \(w\). Lower is better. For this chapter we use a simple bowl-shaped loss so the arithmetic stays clean:

$$ L(w) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,(w - 3)^2 + 1 $$

Here \(w\) is the weight (our one knob), and the curve bottoms out at \(w=3\) — that lowest point is what we are hunting for.

Step 2 — the slope (gradient)

The gradient \(\frac{dL}{dw}\) is just the slope of the curve at the current \(w\): how fast the loss rises as you move right. Differentiating the bowl gives a tidy result:

$$ \frac{dL}{dw} \;=\; (w - 3) $$

If \(w\) is to the right of 3 the slope is positive (loss climbs going right, so go left); to the left it is negative (go right). The slope always points uphill, so we move the opposite way.

Step 3 — the update rule

We step in the downhill direction by a fraction \(\eta\) (the Greek letter eta) of the slope. \(\eta\) is the learning rate — the size of each step:

$$ w \;\leftarrow\; w \;-\; \eta\,\frac{dL}{dw} $$

The minus sign is the whole trick: it turns "uphill slope" into a "downhill step".

Worked example, real numbers. Start at \(w=1\) with learning rate \(\eta=0.5\).
Slope: \(\frac{dL}{dw}=(1-3)=-2\).
Update: \(w \leftarrow 1 - 0.5\times(-2) = 1 + 1 = 2\).
New loss: \(L(2)=\tfrac12(2-3)^2+1 = 0.5+1 = 1.5\) — down from \(L(1)=\tfrac12(1-3)^2+1 = 2+1 = 3\).
Step again from \(w=2\): slope \(=(2-3)=-1\), so \(w \leftarrow 2-0.5(-1)=2.5\), loss \(L(2.5)=\tfrac12(0.5)^2+1=1.125\). Each step lands closer to \(w=3\). That is learning, one honest step at a time.

⚡ Roll the ball downhill

The cyan ball sits on the loss curve. Press step to apply one update; the yellow trail shows its path. Slide the learning rate \(\eta\) and watch the behaviour change: too small crawls, just right glides to the bottom, too big overshoots — and above \(\eta=2\) it diverges and flies off.

Where this shows up in ML

Real models do not have one knob — they have millions. But the rule is identical: the loss measures prediction error, the gradient is computed for every weight at once (by backpropagation), and each weight takes the same downhill step. Training a neural network is this loop, repeated billions of times.

Pitfall — the learning rate is everything. Too small and training takes forever. Too large and the steps overshoot the minimum, bouncing back and forth or even exploding to infinity. In the lab, push \(\eta\) past 2 and watch the loss diverge. Choosing \(\eta\) well is one of the most important practical skills in deep learning.

02 · The building block

The Neuron

A neuron is a tiny decision-maker. It takes a few numbers in, weighs each by how much it cares, adds a bias, and then squashes the total into a yes/no-ish answer between 0 and 1. Stack thousands of them and you have a neural network.

Analogy first. Think of deciding whether to go outside. You weigh a few signals: how sunny it is (you care a lot — big weight), how windy (you mildly dislike it — small negative weight). You add a personal bias (some people just love being out). If the total feeling crosses a threshold, you go. A neuron does precisely this with numbers and a smooth threshold.

Step 1 — the weighted sum

Given two inputs \(x_1, x_2\), the neuron has matching weights \(w_1, w_2\) (how much each input matters) and a single bias \(b\) (a baseline nudge). It forms the weighted sum \(z\):

$$ z \;=\; w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + b $$

Step 2 — the activation

The raw sum \(z\) can be any number. We pass it through the sigmoid function \(\sigma\), which gently squashes anything into the range \((0,1)\) — perfect for reading as a probability or a soft "yes":

$$ y \;=\; \sigma(z) \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} $$

When \(z\) is large and positive, \(y\) is near 1 (a confident yes). When \(z\) is very negative, \(y\) is near 0 (a no). At \(z=0\), \(y=0.5\) — perfectly undecided.

Worked example, real numbers. Let \(w_1=1.5,\; w_2=-1,\; b=0.5\), and feed \(x_1=1,\; x_2=2\).
Weighted sum: \(z = 1.5\times1 + (-1)\times2 + 0.5 = 1.5 - 2 + 0.5 = 0\).
Activation: \(y = \sigma(0) = \dfrac{1}{1+e^{0}} = \dfrac{1}{2} = 0.5\).
So this neuron is exactly on the fence. Nudge \(b\) up to \(1\) and \(z=1\), giving \(y=\sigma(1)=\dfrac{1}{1+e^{-1}}\approx 0.731\) — now it leans "yes".

The decision boundary

The neuron answers "yes" (\(y>0.5\)) exactly when \(z>0\). The line \(w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + b = 0\) splits the input plane into a yes-side and a no-side. Moving the weights tilts that line; moving the bias slides it. The lab below draws this line live.

Where this shows up in ML

One neuron with a sigmoid is logistic regression — a complete classifier on its own. A whole network is just layers of these units, where each layer's outputs become the next layer's inputs. Everything large is built from this small piece.

Pitfall — without the bias, the line is trapped. If \(b=0\), the boundary \(w_1x_1+w_2x_2=0\) must pass through the origin \((0,0)\). The bias is what lets the neuron place its decision line anywhere. Set \(b\) to 0 in the lab and notice the line snaps through the centre.

03 · The shape of randomness

The Normal Distribution

Measure people's heights, sum many small random effects, or look at the errors a model makes — over and over you get the same symmetric hump. This is the Normal (or Gaussian) distribution, the most important curve in all of statistics.

Analogy first. Drop a thousand marbles through a board of pegs (a Galton board). Each marble bounces left or right at every peg, purely by chance. Yet they do not spread out evenly — they pile up in the middle and thin out at the edges, forming a bell. Most outcomes are near the average; extremes are rare. That pile is the Normal distribution.

Step 1 — the two knobs

The whole shape is controlled by just two numbers:

  • \(\mu\) (mu) — the mean: where the peak sits. Slide it and the bell moves left or right.
  • \(\sigma\) (sigma) — the standard deviation: how wide the spread is. Small \(\sigma\) is a tall narrow spike; large \(\sigma\) is a low wide hump.

Step 2 — the formula

The height of the curve at a value \(x\) — the probability density \(f(x)\) — is:

$$ f(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\;\exp\!\left(-\,\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) $$

It looks heavy, but every piece earns its place. The fraction \(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\) measures how many standard deviations \(x\) is from the mean. Squaring it and negating means the height falls off fast as you move away from \(\mu\). The front factor \(\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\) is just bookkeeping so the total area under the curve is exactly 1.

Worked example, real numbers. Take \(\mu=0,\;\sigma=1\) (the "standard" Normal) and ask for the height at \(x=0\).
The exponent is \(-\dfrac{(0-0)^2}{2\cdot1^2}=0\), and \(e^{0}=1\).
So \(f(0)=\dfrac{1}{1\cdot\sqrt{2\pi}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6.2832}}=\dfrac{1}{2.5066}\approx 0.399\).
That \(0.399\) is the height of the peak of the standard bell — the single most important number on the curve.

Step 3 — the 68–95–99.7 rule

Because the shape is fixed, the area (probability) inside bands around the mean is always the same:

$$ \begin{aligned} P(\mu-\sigma < x < \mu+\sigma) &\approx 68\% \\ P(\mu-2\sigma < x < \mu+2\sigma) &\approx 95\% \\ P(\mu-3\sigma < x < \mu+3\sigma) &\approx 99.7\% \end{aligned} $$

So about 2 in 3 values land within one \(\sigma\) of the mean, and almost everything (99.7%) is within three. This is why a "3-sigma event" means "extremely rare".

⚡ Reshape the bell

Slide \(\mu\) to move the peak and \(\sigma\) to squeeze or spread it — the curve redraws live, and the peak height updates to match the formula. Press shade bands to paint the 68–95–99.7 regions and confirm the rule with your own eyes.

Where this shows up in ML

Weights are often initialised from a Normal distribution. Many models assume their errors are Normally distributed — that assumption is exactly what makes "minimise squared error" the right thing to do. And the Central Limit Theorem explains why the bell appears everywhere: sums of many small independent effects tend toward Normal, no matter their individual shapes.

Pitfall — not everything is Normal. Incomes, city sizes, and word frequencies are heavily skewed with long tails — assuming a bell there badly underestimates rare-but-large events. Always look at your data before trusting the 68–95–99.7 rule.

04 · Tying it together

Key Takeaways

Three foundational ideas, each one a piece of how modern machine learning actually works. If these click, a great deal of the rest has a home to land in.

1 · Gradient descent

Learning is walking downhill on a loss curve. Slope gives direction; the learning rate \(\eta\) gives step size. Too big diverges, too small crawls.

2 · The neuron

Weighted sum, plus a bias, squashed by a sigmoid. It draws a decision line you can tilt and slide. Networks are just layers of these.

3 · The Normal distribution

The bell curve from \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\). About 68% within one \(\sigma\), 95% within two, 99.7% within three. The default model of noise.

The thread. A model has weights (idea 2), it learns them by gradient descent (idea 1), and we reason about its uncertainty with the Normal distribution (idea 3).
